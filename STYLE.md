# Translation Style for Cicero by Claude

## The governing principle

Render Cicero in clear, confident modern English at a high register, written
for a reader of expanded vocabulary and intelligence. Stay faithful to the
meaning above all; preserve the shape of his thought, the rhythm of his
clauses, and the architecture of his rhetorical figures. Let Cicero's poetry
come through his structure, not through ornament added in English.

The poetry in the translation should be Cicero's poetry, not the translator's.
When his Latin has rhythm, balance, suspension, or surprise, preserve those
structures. When it is plain, the English is plain. The translator's job is to
be a conduit for his shape, not to add literary effects on top.

## Voice

- Modern English, not archaic. No "thou," no "doth," no inverted Victorian
  word order pretending to be elevated.
- Confident, not hedged. Do not soften assertions the Latin makes confidently.
  Do not insert qualifying adverbs the Latin does not have.
- Direct, not euphemistic. "Stripped me of natural ability," not "took from me
  whatever talent I might claim."
- Match register to genre. Speeches are oratorical and rhythmic; philosophy is
  precise and reflective; letters are intimate, sometimes hurried, often
  anxious. The same voice flexes; it does not fragment.

## Diction

- Trust the reader. If the right English word is "stupefied," "temerity,"
  "deliberation," "insensate," use it. The reader has an expanded vocabulary.
- Use the cognate when it lands cleanly. *Profecto* → "surely." *Temeritas* →
  "temerity" or "recklessness." *Amentissimi* → "deranged" or "insensate."
  Pick by rhythm and weight, not by avoidance of the Latinate root.
- Plain words for plain Latin. When Cicero writes simply — and he often does,
  especially in the letters — translate simply.
- Asseveratives stay strong. *Profecto*, *certe*, *vero*, *nimirum*: render
  with confidence, not with hedges. "Surely," not "in truth."

## Things to preserve

- Cicero's rhetorical figures: anaphora (repeated openings), tricolon
  (three-part lists), antithesis (balanced opposites), parallelism. These are
  the architecture of his prose. When he repeats a word at the head of
  successive clauses, the English repeats it too.
- His sentence shape, where English allows. The build, the suspension, the
  landing. Break for English readability where required, but preserve the
  *function* of the structure.
- His tense distinctions. When he shifts from present to future ("what I am
  doing... what I am about to do"), preserve the shift. Don't flatten to a
  single tense.
- Direct address. When Cicero says *patres conscripti*, render it "senators"
  or "members of the Senate" — not "conscript fathers," which is archaic and
  meaningless to modern readers.
- Roman cultural and religious references in their Roman form. Cicero swears
  *hercle* — "by Hercules," not "by god." He addresses Jupiter, not "the
  Almighty." Romans sacrificed to Roman gods; do not Christianize.

## Idioms

- Translate idioms by function when literal rendering would obscure rather
  than illuminate. *Quod in buccam venerit* — literally "whatever has come up
  onto your tongue" — becomes "whatever first comes to mind." The image is
  lost; the meaning, which is the point, is preserved.
- When the literal image *does* illuminate, keep it. *Quasi libamentis
  decerpsimus* — "as if drawing offerings from each" — is a rich, intelligible
  metaphor in English. Keep it.

## Roman institutions and offices

- Keep in their Latin names where there is no clean English equivalent:
  Senate, Forum, consul, tribune, praetor, aedile, lictor, Comitium, Rostra,
  princeps senatus.
- Translate names of laws on first occurrence with a parenthetical, then use
  the Latin: "the Lex Plautia (a law on public violence)..."

## Letters

- Open with a standardized dateline:

  > *To Atticus, at Rome*
  > *Formiae, 18 January 49 BC*

- Letters are often elliptical, full of inside references. Translate what is
  there; do not expand. The headnote can supply context.
- Greek phrases (which Cicero sprinkles throughout the letters) should be
  rendered in English with a footnote: *[Greek: ...]*.

## Things to avoid

- Footnoting in the translation itself. Context goes in the headnote.
- Bracketed translator's notes in the body. If a passage requires
  explanation, the headnote handles it.
- Echoing famous existing translations of canonical lines. Render the Latin
  freshly. Famous renderings are sometimes natural English shapes for the
  Latin and sometimes accidents of one translator's phrasing that have stuck.
  Distinguish, and translate from the Latin.

## Section numbering

- Preserve the standard section numbers used in scholarly editions (Teubner,
  Oxford Classical Texts). These are how the work is cited.
