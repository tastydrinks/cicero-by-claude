\chapter*{Translator's preface}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Translator's preface}
\markboth{Translator's preface}{Translator's preface}

\noindent
This preface will, in the finished book, set out the principles by which the
translation has been carried out --- a fuller exposition of what the working
file \texttt{STYLE.md} captures in compressed form. Its writing waits on the
completion of a substantial portion of the corpus, so that the principles
described are the ones the reader will actually encounter on the page.

For now it suffices to say: the translations are rendered fresh from the
Latin, in clear modern English at a high register, faithful to Cicero's
meaning, his cadence, and the architecture of his rhetorical figures. The
poetry on the page is his, not the translator's.

\bigskip

\chapter*{Why this exists}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Why this exists}
\markboth{Why this exists}{Why this exists}

\noindent
A complete chronological Cicero in English does not exist as a single
volume. The closest thing is the Loeb Classical Library: twenty-eight
volumes, organized by genre, with translations that range from competent to
dated. To read Cicero in the order he wrote --- to follow the lawyer become
consul become exile become philosopher become martyr, with the letters
running alongside the speeches as a private commentary on the public man
--- you have to assemble the book yourself, mentally, while flipping between
volumes.

I wanted that book to exist. Not as a scholarly edition. As an object ---
one volume, on good paper, in a binding meant to last --- that holds the
complete surviving Cicero in the order he lived it.

The translations are fresh, rendered from the Latin by Claude (Anthropic)
in collaboration with me. Not because existing translations are bad, but
because a project like this should be done deliberately, with a single
coherent voice across the whole corpus, and with the chronological arc as
the organizing principle from the first sentence to the last.

If it ends up being only mine, that's enough. If others find use in it,
better still. Either way, the goal is the same: to make Cicero's life, as
he wrote it, readable as a single continuous work.

\bigskip
\begin{flushright}
\itshape{Alexander Woods}\\
{\itshape\small 2026}
\end{flushright}
