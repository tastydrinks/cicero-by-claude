\ciceroLetterOpener{TVLLIVS TIRONI S.}

\ciceroSection{1} I see what you are up to: you want your own letters too collected into volumes. But come now --- you, who are usually the \textit{[Greek: kan\=on]}, the standard, of my writing --- where does that bit of \textit{[Greek: akuron]}, that loose usage, come from: ``serving your health faithfully''? How did ``faithfully'' end up in that place? The word's own proper home is duty; it has many migrations into other territories --- learning has its faith, so does a house, an art, even a field. ``Faithful'' \textit{can} be said this way, provided it is, as Theophrastus would have it, a modest metaphor. But more of this in person.

\ciceroSection{2} Demetrius has come to me; from his entourage at least I have rather neatly \textit{[Greek: aph\=omil\=esa]} ---  prised myself loose. You, of course, were not in a position to see him. He will be here tomorrow, so you will see him then; for I am thinking of leaving here the morning after. Your health is a serious worry to me. Put yourself at its service; do everything that needs doing. Then count yourself as being with me; then count yourself as giving me satisfaction in full measure. Thank you for the trouble you have taken over Cuspius; I want very much to do well by him. Farewell.
