% Ad Familiares 6.3 --- headnote
% ad-familiares-06-03, beginning of January 45 BC, Rome

\textit{Cicero to A. Manlius Torquatus, written at Rome at the
opening of January 45 BC (Perseus: \textit{Romae in.~m.~Ian.}).
Torquatus is still away in Athens; the African war is over (Thapsus
the previous April) and the Spanish campaign against the
younger Pompey and Labienus is under way, with the decisive
engagement at Munda still some weeks off. The letter pairs with
\textit{Fam.}~6.4 from the same month and is closer in voice to
the philosophical compression of those years' \textit{Tusculans}
than the earlier 6.1 had been.}

\textit{The argument is the bare-bones Stoic-Epicurean doublet
that will recur across the consolatory cluster: in life nothing
can harm us so long as we are without fault, and in death we
have no perception by which to be harmed. Cicero stops himself
at \S 4 with a wry self-correction in Greek --- \textit{glauk'
eis Athenas}, ``owls to Athens,'' the proverb for sending
something where it is already in surplus --- pointed for a
recipient who is actually in Athens and who hardly needs Cicero
to teach him the philosophical commonplaces he can hear under
his window. The closing return to the private register
(``you, and yours, and all that is yours, are my deepest care,
and will be while I live'') is the same valedictory shape as
\textit{Fam.}~6.1.7.}
