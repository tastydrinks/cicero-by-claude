% Ad Quintum Fratrem 1.3 --- headnote
% ad-quintum-fratrem-01-03, 13 June 58 BC, written at Thessalonica

\textit{Cicero to his brother Quintus, written from Thessalonica
on the Ides of June 58 BC, sent on the same day as
\textit{Att.} 3.9. The companion letter to that one: Quintus,
returning from his Asian governorship, had been sent on to Rome
without their meeting. The note Cicero feared he had not sent ---
the brothers' boys had reached Quintus without a letter from
him --- this letter answers, opening with the famous triple
vocative \textit{mi frater, mi frater, mi frater} and the
incredulous question whether Quintus could have thought he was
angry. The reasoning of \textsection 1 turns into the bitterest
self-rebuke of the surviving correspondence: he had not wanted
to be seen, not the other way around, because what Quintus would
have seen would not have been his brother but ``a kind of
breathing effigy of a dead man.''}

\textit{The letter widens, in \textsection 3, into the most
sustained roll-call of those Cicero misses --- the brother
near his own age, the daughter Tullia, the boy Marcus already
old enough to feel what was happening, the brother's own son
his nephew, and Terentia held back at Rome to look after
``the remains of our common disaster, the children of our two
houses.'' \textsection 5 declares the bargain that holds
Cicero alive --- ``as long as you have need of me, I shall
live'' --- and \textsection 7 reveals, with his usual
exactitude in money matters, that he is being kept up by
Quintus's resources and not his own. \textsection 8 names
Hortensius and Q. Arrius as the men whose pretended advice
brought him down; \textsection 9 names Pompey a
\textit{simulator}. The letter closes with the weeping that
will not let him write, and the prayer for ``the power of
dying in my country.''}
