% Ad Atticum 14.2 --- headnote
% ad-atticum-14-02, 8 April 44 BC, suburban villa of Matius

\textit{Cicero to Atticus, written on 8 April 44 BC, still at the
suburban villa of Matius --- Perseus dateline \textit{Scr. in
suburbano Mati vi Id. Apr. a. 710 (44)}. The day after the
Matius interview, Cicero is reading Atticus's two letters from
Rome: applause in the theatre for Lucius Cassius (one of the
Liberators' connections), and what is evidently encouraging
news about the actor Publilius Syrus and the temper of the
crowd. These are the ``good signs of a public mind in agreement
with itself'' that Cicero will keep watching through April:
whether the city, gathered into a theatre, can be seen to side
with the deed of the Ides.}

\textit{The middle of the letter circles a corrected anecdote
from the previous day --- Cicero now repeating Caesar's remark
about him in Brutus's antechamber, with Brutus, not himself,
identified as the \textit{[Greek: phalakrōma]} (``bald-pate'')
who is the deadly enemy of \textit{otium}. The Perseus text
carries daggered cruxes around the line that introduces the
joke, preserved here, and a doublet of the Greek tag
(\textit{φαλάκωμα} / \textit{φαλάκρωμα}) which the cruxes
acknowledge. The closing is the itinerary that runs through
these post-Ides days: Tusculum today, Lanuvium tomorrow, then
Astura --- with the standing courtesy to Pilia and Atticus's
daughter Attica.}
