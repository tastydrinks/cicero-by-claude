% Ad Familiares 13.10 --- headnote
% ad-familiares-13-10, beginning of 46 BC, Rome

\textit{Cicero to M. Junius Brutus, written at Rome at the
beginning of 46 BC (Perseus: \textit{Romae in.~a.~708 (46)}).
This is the first piece in the long book of recommendations
addressed to Brutus, who at this date was governor of Cisalpine
Gaul under Caesar. Cicero is commending to him his own quaestor,
M. Terentius Varro Gibba --- a young man who has grown up in
Cicero's friendship, who has been one of the literary circle
(\textit{hoc studio nostro}, the studies of oratory and letters
that Cicero keeps central even now), who took losses in the
public-revenue companies, served honourably on both sides of the
advocate's bench, and stood for office before the war
intervened.}

\textit{The letter does what a Roman recommendation is supposed
to do, with the deliberate doubling that marks the genre at its
best. The opening move is institutional: the quaestorship is a
bond like that of son to father, and Terentius does not strictly
need a letter at all. The body then supplies what the
institutional tie cannot --- a biography of long acquaintance,
intellectual common ground, the test of carrying confidential
letters from Cicero to Caesar at Brundisium, a survey of
character. The close returns to the institutional opening with a
graceful epigram: in any new connection it matters by what
recommendation the door of friendship is first opened, and that
is the work of this letter. The piece is the standard against
which the rest of book 13 wants to be read.}
