% Ad Atticum 13.27 --- headnote
% ad-atticum-13-27, 25 May 45 BC, Tusculanum

\textit{Cicero to Atticus, written at the Tusculan villa on 25 May
45 BC --- Perseus dateline \textit{Scr. in Tusculano viii K. Iun.
a. 709 (45)}. Mid-cluster in the Tusculan daily-letter run of late
May and early June: the \textit{Academica} revision and the
dedication-shuffle around Varro continue in the background, but
this letter and its sequel 13.28 are dominated by Cicero's
gathering second thoughts about the letter of political advice
to Caesar that Atticus had urged him to compose.}

\textit{Caesar's intimates have now read the draft and have come
back with so many demanded alterations that Cicero seizes on
their reaction as a way out of the whole project. The texture is
candid relief: he had set out to flatter and he knows it, the
substance was hollow, and to send the thing now --- after long
silence and on the eve of a Parthian campaign --- would only
expose him further. Five Greek phrases mark the joints of the
self-justification: \textit{kolakeia} (``flattery,'' the honest
name for what the letter was); \textit{epiteugma} and
\textit{apoteugma} (``success'' and ``failure,'' a balanced pair
weighed against the risk); \textit{parakinduneuein} (``running the
risk''); and \textit{meiligma} (``sop'' or ``placation,'' Cicero's
fear that the letter would be read as making amends for his
\textit{Cato}); with \textit{spoud\=e} (``serious pains'') at the
close. Section 2 turns abruptly to property business --- gardens
for the shrine, the Faberius debt, an auction day --- and ends
with a courier passed on for news about Atticus's daughter
Attica.}
