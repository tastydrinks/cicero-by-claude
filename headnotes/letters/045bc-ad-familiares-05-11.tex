% Ad Familiares 5.11 --- headnote
% ad-familiares-05-11, end of October 45 BC, Rome

\textit{Cicero in Rome to Publius Vatinius, imperator on
campaign in Illyricum, written at the end of October 45 BC
(Perseus: \textit{Romae ex.~m.~Oct.~a.~709 (45)}). The letter
is one half of the surviving two-way correspondence with
Vatinius preserved in book 5 of the \textit{Ad Familiares}; the
incoming letters from Vatinius's camp at Narona stand alongside
it (5.9, 5.10) and put Cicero's replies in unusual relief.
The pair were once political enemies --- Vatinius the Caesarian
tribune of 59 BC, the target of \textit{In Vatinium} --- but
since Cicero's reluctant defence of him in 54 BC at Caesar's
behest the relationship has run, on both sides, on the warm
courtesies of a settled patron-client friendship.}

\textit{The letter answers three requests evidently made in
Vatinius's previous despatch. First (\S 1), Cicero accepts
Vatinius's thanks for the favour done him in Rome and assures
him of the same readiness in everything else. Second (\S 2), he
takes up the commendation of Vatinius's wife Pompeia: he has
already spoken to Sura on her behalf, will see to whatever she
needs in person if required, and asks Vatinius to instruct her
to treat nothing as too trivial. Third (\S 3), he is brisk
about Dionysius --- presumably a fugitive slave or freedman
Vatinius is hunting --- with the jocular promise that any
scoundrel will be led captive in Vatinius's triumph, and closes
with the soldier's curse on the Dalmatians and the assurance,
echoed back from Vatinius's own dispatch, that they will be
taken and will lend lustre to his \textit{res gestae}. The
register throughout is the easy, slightly comic warmth that
Cicero reserves for old enemies turned friends, with the
promise of a \textit{supplicatio} and an eventual triumph in
the air on both sides.}
