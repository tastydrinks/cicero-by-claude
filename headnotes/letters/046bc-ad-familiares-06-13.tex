% Ad Familiares 6.13 --- headnote
% ad-familiares-06-13, early August 46 BC, Rome

\textit{Cicero to Quintus Ligarius, written at Rome in early
August 46 BC (works.yaml \texttt{-0046-08-15} at month-precision;
the Perseus dateline reads \textit{Romae in.~m.~Sext.~a.~708 (46)},
``at the beginning of the month of Sextilis'' --- a tightening to
\textit{circa} ${-}0046$-$08$-$01$ is plausible, but
month-precision covers it). Ligarius, who had been left in
command in Africa under the elder Considius and was caught up in
the Pompeian remnant under Scipio and Cato that Caesar destroyed
at Thapsus in April 46, is the addressee whose case Cicero argues
the following year before Caesar himself in the speech
\textit{Pro Ligario}. He is in exile when this letter is written,
his recall not yet secured; the despatches from Africa have only
just come in, and Cicero, in company with Ligarius's brothers
(Titus and Publius), has begun the campaign at Rome.}

\textit{No Greek. The letter is short, working, and tightly
keyed to \textit{Fam.}~6.10 of the same month --- the line
\textit{si tantum possem quantum in ea re~p., de qua ita sum
meritus} is the same self-deprecating formula about Cicero's
diminished \textit{gratia} that opens 6.10.2, and the doctrinal
pivot at the close (\textit{conscientia et factorum et consiliorum
tuorum}, ``the consciousness of what you have done and meant'')
is the standing Stoic-consolatio formula of the Pompeian-exile
cluster. The structural beat ``first I shall write what I see;
then I shall show what I want by the deed, not by speech'' is the
shape of the letter: section~2 the survey, section~3 the realism
about Caesar's anger over the African resistance, section~4 the
pledge of work for the brothers, section~5 the bravery-charge ---
a four-part architecture inside five short paragraphs.}
