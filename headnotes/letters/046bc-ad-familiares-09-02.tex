% Ad Familiares 9.2 --- headnote
% ad-familiares-09-02, 20 April 46 BC, Rome

\textit{Cicero to Varro, written at Rome a little after the
twelfth day before the Kalends of May 46 BC (Perseus:
\textit{Romae paulo post xii K.~Mai.~a.~708 (46)}), that is, in
the days just after the news of Thapsus had reached the city
and Caesar's victory over the last Pompeian field army in Africa
was being celebrated. The light comic opening --- the friend
Caninius who keeps missing his rides, the night's letter that
goes stale before its courier appears --- gives way at once to
the political question that organizes the whole sequence: how
two of the more conspicuous Pompeians left alive at Rome should
hold themselves while the triumph is being prepared. Cicero's
advice is the great Epicurean tag in Latin dress: ``let us avoid
men's eyes if we cannot so easily avoid their tongues.'' Those
who won look on us as conquered; those who lost are sorry we are
still alive. He gives the candid account of why he has stayed:
to leave Rome would have invited every uncharitable construction
imaginable, and the daily habit of staying has, almost without
his noticing, grown a callus.}

\textit{The close is the manifesto of the literary turn. They
will live together in their studies, from which they used to
seek mere enjoyment but from which they now seek their safety.
They will not refuse anyone who wants them in the work of
rebuilding the commonwealth, not merely as architects but as
common builders; and if no one takes them up, they will write and
read of \textit{politeiai} --- of constitutions --- and ``in our
writing and our books, as the most learned of the ancients did,
attend to the commonwealth and inquire into customs and laws.''
This sentence is the design-sketch for what becomes, over the
next eighteen months, the most concentrated burst of philosophy
in Latin: the \textit{Paradoxa Stoicorum}, the \textit{Brutus},
the \textit{Orator}, eventually the \textit{De Finibus} and
\textit{Tusculans}. Greek phrases are denser here than anywhere
else in Cicero's correspondence (\textit{heōlos}, ``stale''; the
adverb \textit{lelēthotōs}, ``imperceptibly''; \textit{politeiai}
for \textit{De Re Publica}-style constitutional inquiry); the
register acknowledges Varro as a fellow Hellenist of the highest
rank.}
