% Ad Familiares 7.28 --- headnote
% ad-familiares-07-28, August 46 BC, Rome

\textit{Cicero to M'.\ Curius, written from Rome early in August 46
BC. Curius is a friend long settled at Patrae in Achaea --- a Roman
businessman, by the warmth of the address an old acquaintance ---
and the letter is a reflection on exile, friendship, and how to
live in a Republic that has ceased to be one. Cicero opens by
confessing the inversion of his earlier judgement: he used to think
Curius was out of his mind to choose Patrae and the Peloponnese over
the city; now Curius seems to him the one who saw clearly.}

\textit{Section 2 turns on a tag of Ennius's \textit{Thyestes} ---
``where neither the Pelopids'' (the rest is left to the reader's ear:
\textit{neither the deeds nor the fame of the Pelopids shall I
hear}) --- and matches Curius's geographical retreat to Cicero's own
inward one. Cicero is reachable at the morning \textit{salutatio},
where like-minded callers now seem a \textit{rara avis}, but the rest
of the day he buries in the library, where he is producing work of
some quantity --- the second great burst of philosophical writing
that 46 BC began. The third section makes the elegy explicit: the
\textit{res publica}, dearer to him than life on the strength of
their mutual kindness, has collapsed; the fault he refuses to lay
on the one man in whose power everything stands (with the dry
parenthesis that the fact of any such concentration was already
the wrong), but on circumstance and on themselves. Cicero closes
with the friendship-letter's poise: ``wisely you abandoned this
scene, if by deliberation; happily, if by chance.'' The Perseus
dateline is \textit{in.\ m.\ Sext.\ a.\ 708 (46)}, early August;
the meta entry's \textit{-0046-08-15} mid-month stamp is retained
as month-precision.}
