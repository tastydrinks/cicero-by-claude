% Ad Atticum 2.3 --- headnote
% ad-atticum-02-03, December 60 BC, written from a country villa

\textit{Cicero to Atticus, written from a country villa in
December 60 BC, on the eve of Caesar's consulship. Three
pieces. First (\textsection 1) the news that the elder
Lucius Valerius (the man whose patron in 59 will be Cicero
in \textit{Pro Flacco}'s circle) has been acquitted with
Hortensius defending --- a trial allegedly bought by
Pompey's man Aulus's son Afranius. Second (\textsection 2)
the famous architectural joke: the architect Cyrus has built
narrow windows into Cicero's house, and Atticus has objected;
Cicero defends Cyrus by quoting the architect's own
explanation, parodied with a small geometrical proof in
Greek (``let A be the eye, B the visible object, D and E the
rays\ldots'') --- the cultivated bilingualism of the corpus
at its best.}

\textit{Third (\textsection 3--4) the great political
question of January 59 BC: how should Cicero respond to
the new consul Caesar's agrarian bill? Three options ---
brave resistance (full of praise but with struggle); quiet
withdrawal (``not unlike going off to Solonium or Antium''
to one of the country villas); positive support, which is
what Caesar expects (the visit from Cornelius Balbus,
Caesar's intimate, is reported here for the first time, as
the visible point of the new combination of Pompey,
Caesar, and Crassus). What is on the table is a friendship
with all three, peace with the multitude, ``the leisure of
old age''; but Cicero is held back by the famous closing
sentence of his own consular epic ---``Meanwhile keep, and
increase, the courses you sought from your youth, and as
consul, by virtue and spirit'' --- and by Hector's tag
from the Iliad: ``one omen is best, to fight for one's
country.'' The answer is left for the walks at the
forthcoming Compitalia (the cross-road festival), where
Atticus is invited.}
