% Post Reditum in Senatu --- headnote
% post-reditum-in-senatu, 5 September 57 BC, Rome

\textit{Delivered in the Senate on 5 September 57 BC --- the
day after Cicero's return from exile, the centuriate
assembly of 4 August having voted his recall and Cicero
having reached Rome on 4 September up the steps of the
Capitol with all Italy at his back. The opening word
\textit{si} (``if'') begins the unbroken chain of
\textit{vestra in nos universa promerita} that runs through
the first two sections; the speech is a sustained
\textit{gratiarum actio}, with no narrative element except
what is needed to ground the gratitude. The architecture is
a roll-call: the Senate's constancy through 58 BC
(\textsection 3--4), the new year of the consul P. Lentulus
Spinther and the support of Q. Metellus Nepos and Cn. Pompeius
(\textsection 5--6, 25--29), the courage of the tribunes
T. Annius Milo and P. Sestius (\textsection 19--20, 30), the
roll-call of the other tribunes and praetors of 57 BC
(\textsection 21--23). The two great invective sections at
the centre, on the consuls of 58 --- A. Gabinius the
curled-haired Epicurean and L. Calpurnius Piso Caesoninus the
philosophy-flecked dullard (\textsection 10--18) --- are
among the most savagely written passages in the surviving
oratory, and would be answered the next year in
\textit{In Pisonem}. \textsection 32--34 lay out Cicero's
reading of his own withdrawal: that to defend himself by arms
would have meant a second civil war on top of the first, and
that, since the commonwealth had gone with him, the
commonwealth's recall has brought him back again. The closing
\textsection 36 is the public undertaking that this speech
exists to make: that the recovery has bought no diminution of
his old liberty of speech --- only an enlargement.}
