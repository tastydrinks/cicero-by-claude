% Pro M. Tullio --- headnote
% pro-tullio, 71 BC

\textit{Delivered at Rome in 71 BC, surviving in extensive but incomplete
fragments. A civil action under a recently introduced praetorian formula
of M. Lucullus that gave a quadruple-damages remedy against any household
which by force, with armed men, did damage by malicious design --- a remedy
crafted to address the late-Republican plague of armed slave-bands roaming
the great estates. Marcus Tullius (no relation to Cicero, though Cicero
notes the shared name affectionately in section 4) had inherited an estate
in Thurium; his neighbour Publius Fabius, who had bought an adjoining
estate from C. Claudius, sent his slaves armed by night to butcher
Tullius's slaves on a disputed boundary lot. The defence (carried by
Lucius Quinctius) admitted the killing but contended the formula required
proof of "wrongfulness," which the praetor had deliberately omitted. Most
of the speech is a sustained argument that the omission was deliberate
and that the formula leaves the defendant nowhere to stand. The opening
of the case (the sponsio, the assessment) and the close (verdict and
peroration) are lost.}
