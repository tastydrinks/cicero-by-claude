% In Catilinam II --- headnote
% in-catilinam-2, 9 November 63 BC

\textit{The second Catilinarian, delivered on the day after
the first --- a \textit{contio} in the Forum announcing
Catiline's flight from Rome the previous night and explaining
to the people what had happened in the Senate.}

\textit{The opening peals out the news in four near-synonymous
verbs and gives the speech its mood: ``He has gone, he has
departed, he has escaped, he has burst out''
(\textit{abiit, excessit, evasit, erupit}). Catiline is gone;
the open war is the war we wanted; the dagger is no longer at
our ribs in the Senate-house. The body of the speech is the
famous typology of the conspirators left behind, sorted into
six classes: the heavily indebted but well-landed who would
rather sacrifice the commonwealth than mortgage an estate;
the would-be dictators who think a chance of rule is worth
the city's ash; the bankrupted Sullan settlers who would have
to raise Sulla from the dead to be solvent; the chronic
defaulters; the parricides and cut-throats Cicero says he
does not bother to summon back; and last, peculiar to
Catiline, the ``praetorian cohort of harlots'' --- combed,
glossy, scented, draped in long-sleeved tunics and gauze
veils, tutored to brandish daggers and scatter poisons, who
will (Cicero asks) presumably bring their little women along
with them to the Apennine snows. The closing pivot is the
oratorical antithesis of the speech: on this side modesty,
purity, faith, piety, constancy, honour, continence, equity,
temperance, courage, prudence; on the other wantonness,
debauchery, fraud, crime, madness, baseness, lust, iniquity,
luxury, sloth, recklessness. Even if men's zeal should fail,
the gods themselves would compel so many vices to be overcome
by these virtues.}

\textit{The promise that closes the speech --- that no good
man shall perish, that, by the punishment of a few, all may
be safe --- will be tested twenty-six days later, when the
arrest of the Allobroges' envoys at the Mulvian Bridge brings
the conspirators in the city into Cicero's hand and forces
the question of their punishment.}
