% Post Reditum ad Quirites --- headnote
% post-reditum-ad-quirites, 7 September 57 BC, Rome

\textit{Delivered to the Roman people in a public meeting on
7 September 57 BC, two days after \textit{Post Reditum in
Senatu}. The senatorial speech is a roll-call of the men who
had brought him back; this speech, addressed to the assembled
citizens of Rome, draws a different shape, the
self-presentation of an exiled magistrate as a
\textit{vir devotus} who at the great moment had pledged
himself to the public good. \textsection 1 is the
\textit{devotio} declaration: that, when he gave himself up
out of the city, he had asked of Jupiter Best and Greatest a
sempiternal punishment if his withdrawal had been for any
private cause; the proof that the prayer was answered as he
asked is in his being now back. \textsection 2--4 work the
paradox that what was lost has now been recovered with the
sweetness made keener by the loss --- children, brother,
fortunes, friendships, the country itself. \textsection 6--8
are the contrast with the recall of P. Popilius Laenas and
Q. Caecilius Metellus Numidicus: those returns had needed a
great train of named kinsmen; for Cicero, ``naked of
kindred,'' the case was carried by his son-in-law C. Piso
Frugi and by his brother Quintus alone. The latter, with the
old line that he ``denied it was right to be separated from
me, not only in dwelling-place but even in tomb.''}

\textit{\textsection 11--16 follow the senatorial speech in
naming Lentulus Spinther as the recall's principal mover, with
the bargain over the provinces of 58 BC named for what it was
--- the price for which the consuls Piso and Gabinius had
sold his case. \textsection 17 closes the catalogue with the
remarkable testimony of L. Gellius Publicola the censorian
that, had Cicero not been consul in 63, ``the commonwealth
would have utterly perished.'' \textsection 19--20 are the
parallel and contrast with C. Marius's recall in 87 BC: a
fellow-Arpinate ``of the same fated necessity'' who had
returned by the sword; Cicero shall use ``what I have been
used to --- speech.'' \textsection 21 names the four classes
of men who wronged him: bitter enemies of the commonwealth;
treacherous friends; the envious; and the traffickers in
provinces, the consuls of 58. The closing \textsection 25 is
that the citizens have judged Rome herself unable to keep her
dignity until they had recovered him.}
